# Clyros Tech Private Limited
## Voice, Tone & Language Rules

**Document Classification:** Internal Use Only  
**Audience:** All personnel creating external-facing content  
**Purpose:** Define how Clyros Tech communicates in writing  
**Last Updated:** January 2026

---

## 1. VOICE CHARACTERISTICS

Voice is the consistent personality and style that defines Clyros Tech communication. Voice does NOT change based on audience or context.

### 1.1 Approved Voice Characteristics

**Professional**  
We communicate as subject matter experts addressing other professionals. No casual language, jokes, or entertainment. We are not your friend; we are your technical partner.

**Precise**  
We use specific, concrete language. Numbers, technical terms, and factual statements preferred over abstract concepts and aspirational language.

**Conservative**  
We understate rather than overstate. Claims are measured and verifiable. We do not hype, exaggerate, or use superlatives.

**Transparent**  
We acknowledge limitations, constraints, and trade-offs. We explain what we do NOT offer as clearly as what we do.

**Calm**  
We avoid urgency language, exclamation marks, and emotional appeals. Decisions should be based on facts, not pressure.

### 1.2 Prohibited Voice Characteristics

**❌ Casual or Conversational**  
No: "Hey! Let's chat about your cloud journey!"  
Yes: "Contact us to discuss cloud migration requirements."

**❌ Promotional or Sales-y**  
No: "Amazing solutions that transform businesses!"  
Yes: "Enterprise software development and cloud infrastructure services."

**❌ Trendy or Buzzword-Heavy**  
No: "Leveraging synergies to disrupt the paradigm with innovative solutions."  
Yes: "Structured delivery methodology focused on measurable outcomes."

**❌ Emotional or Aspirational**  
No: "Imagine a world where your systems just work!"  
Yes: "We build systems designed for reliability and operational accountability."

## 2. TONE GUIDELINES BY AUDIENCE

Tone is how voice adapts to specific contexts and audiences. While voice remains consistent, tone flexes based on who we're addressing.

### 2.1 CXO / Executive Audience

**Context:** Board presentations, executive proposals, C-suite communications

**Tone Characteristics:**
- Businesslike and strategic
- Focused on outcomes and risk
- Minimal technical jargon (explain when necessary)
- Emphasis on governance and accountability

**Example:**  
"Clyros Tech structures engagements to provide executive visibility into progress, risks, and delivery outcomes. Governance frameworks include weekly status reporting, formal change control, and escalation procedures for critical issues."

### 2.2 Procurement / Legal Audience

**Context:** RFP responses, contract negotiations, compliance inquiries

**Tone Characteristics:**
- Extremely precise and literal
- Focused on compliance, liability, and process
- No marketing language whatsoever
- Clear delineation of responsibilities

**Example:**  
"Clyros Tech Private Limited is not certified under HIPAA, GDPR, or SOC 2 frameworks. We design delivery practices to support client compliance obligations as defined in data processing agreements and engagement-specific security requirements."

### 2.3 Technical / Architecture Audience

**Context:** Technical proposals, architecture reviews, engineering discussions

**Tone Characteristics:**
- Technical depth appropriate to audience
- Focus on trade-offs and design decisions
- Specificity about technologies and methodologies
- Acknowledgment of complexity and constraints

**Example:**  
"For high-availability requirements, we recommend active-active multi-region deployment with global load balancing. This introduces data consistency challenges that require either eventual consistency patterns or distributed transaction coordination, each with distinct trade-offs."

### 2.4 Engineering Candidates

**Context:** Job descriptions, careers page, interview process communications

**Tone Characteristics:**
- Serious and substantive
- Emphasis on responsibility and accountability
- Clear about expectations and standards
- No hype about "culture" or "perks"

**Example:**  
"Clyros Tech engineers take ownership of code quality, operational readiness, and client outcomes. This is not an environment for those seeking mentorship, hand-holding, or junior-level learning opportunities. We hire experienced professionals who can operate autonomously in complex technical contexts."

### 2.5 Partner Organizations

**Context:** Partnership inquiries, joint delivery proposals, alliance discussions

**Tone Characteristics:**
- Collaborative but businesslike
- Focus on mutual value and governance
- Clear about capabilities and limitations
- Emphasis on joint accountability

**Example:**  
"Clyros Tech structures partnership engagements with clear roles, responsibilities, and governance mechanisms. Joint delivery requires aligned processes, shared risk management, and transparent client communication. We discuss these frameworks before committing to partnership arrangements."

## 3. LANGUAGE TO PREFER

### 3.1 Concrete and Specific

**Prefer:**
- "We deliver enterprise software development services"
- "Our engineers have 5-15 years of experience"
- "We serve clients in healthcare, financial services, and enterprise SaaS"
- "Delivery aligned with ISO-grade practices"

**Why:** Specificity builds credibility and sets accurate expectations.

### 3.2 Measured and Factual

**Prefer:**
- "Clyros Tech supports international clients through distributed delivery models"
- "We structure engagements to accommodate client timezone requirements"
- "Engineering standards include code review, automated testing, and documentation"
- "We implement security controls appropriate to data sensitivity"

**Why:** Measured language positions us as reliable and trustworthy.

### 3.3 Transparent About Limitations

**Prefer:**
- "We do not maintain physical offices in every market we serve"
- "This is not suitable for organizations seeking low-cost offshore staffing"
- "Clyros Tech is not certified under HIPAA or SOC 2 frameworks"
- "Some jurisdictions may require legal consultation before engagement"

**Why:** Transparency prevents misalignment and builds long-term trust.

### 3.4 Process-Oriented

**Prefer:**
- "We follow structured delivery methodology with defined phases"
- "Governance includes weekly status reporting and risk escalation procedures"
- "Quality gates include code review, automated testing, and security scanning"
- "Knowledge transfer occurs through documentation and structured handoff sessions"

**Why:** Process focus demonstrates operational maturity and reduces client risk perception.

## 4. LANGUAGE TO AVOID

### 4.1 Startup Hype and Buzzwords

**❌ AVOID:**
- "Disrupt," "transform," "revolutionize," "game-changing," "cutting-edge"
- "Rockstar," "ninja," "guru," "wizard" (engineer titles)
- "Synergy," "leverage," "paradigm shift," "best-in-class"
- "Thought leader," "visionary," "trailblazer," "pioneer"

**Why:** These terms undermine credibility with enterprise buyers and mature technical audiences.

### 4.2 Superlatives and Absolutes

**❌ AVOID:**
- "Best," "fastest," "most reliable," "industry-leading," "world-class"
- "Always," "never," "guaranteed," "100% uptime," "zero defects"
- "Unlimited," "any industry," "all technologies," "complete solution"

**Why:** Superlatives are unprovable and create liability. Enterprise buyers are skeptical of absolute claims.

### 4.3 Vague or Abstract Language

**❌ AVOID:**
- "Innovative solutions that drive business value"
- "Comprehensive approach to digital transformation"
- "End-to-end services across the full stack"
- "Holistic engagement model focused on outcomes"

**Why:** Vague language says nothing concrete and sounds like marketing fluff.

### 4.4 Urgency or Pressure Language

**❌ AVOID:**
- "Act now," "limited time," "don't miss out," "urgent need"
- "Falling behind competitors," "rapidly changing landscape"
- Excessive exclamation marks (!!!)
- Countdown timers or artificial scarcity

**Why:** Enterprise decisions are deliberate. Pressure tactics damage credibility.

### 4.5 Emotional or Aspirational Language

**❌ AVOID:**
- "Imagine a future where..."
- "Passionate about technology"
- "We love helping our clients succeed"
- "Dream team," "amazing culture," "exciting opportunity"

**Why:** Professional technical services are evaluated on capability, not emotion.

### 4.6 Unverifiable Claims

**❌ AVOID:**
- "Proven track record" (unless specific metrics provided)
- "Trusted by Fortune 500" (unless clients can be named)
- "Award-winning" (unless specific award cited)
- "Market leader" (unless third-party data supports)

**Why:** Unverifiable claims create legal risk and damage trust if challenged.

## 5. WRITING EXAMPLES

### 5.1 Homepage Hero Section

**❌ BAD:**
"Welcome to Clyros Tech! We're passionate innovators transforming businesses through cutting-edge technology solutions. Our world-class team of experts delivers game-changing results that drive success. Let's revolutionize your digital future together!"

**✅ GOOD:**
"Clyros Tech Private Limited delivers enterprise-grade software development, cloud infrastructure, and technical consulting services. We specialize in mission-critical systems for regulated industries where reliability and security are non-negotiable."

**Why it's good:**
- Specific about what we do
- No buzzwords or hype
- Sets clear expectations
- Professional and measured tone

### 5.2 Service Description

**❌ BAD:**
"Our innovative cloud solutions leverage cutting-edge technologies to unlock unprecedented value and drive digital transformation at scale. We provide end-to-end excellence that delivers amazing results!"

**✅ GOOD:**
"Cloud architecture and engineering services include infrastructure-as-code implementation, Kubernetes orchestration, cloud-native application design, and migration planning. We work with AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud Platform based on client requirements."

**Why it's good:**
- Specific technologies and deliverables
- No empty marketing language
- Sets clear scope
- Verifiable and concrete

### 5.3 Case Study Introduction

**❌ BAD:**
"We helped this amazing client achieve incredible success through our innovative approach! The results exceeded all expectations and transformed their entire business!"

**✅ GOOD:**
"Regional healthcare provider required HIPAA-compliant migration from on-premises infrastructure to AWS. Challenge included zero-downtime requirement and strict data protection controls."

**Why it's good:**
- Factual and specific
- No exaggeration
- Clear problem statement
- Sets up technical discussion

### 5.4 Job Description Opening

**❌ BAD:**
"Join our awesome team of rockstar engineers! We're a fast-paced, dynamic startup disrupting the tech industry. Amazing perks, unlimited PTO, and a culture you'll love!"

**✅ GOOD:**
"Clyros Tech seeks senior backend engineers with 5+ years of production experience in distributed systems. This role requires ownership of code quality, operational readiness, and client delivery outcomes. Not suitable for junior engineers or those seeking mentorship-focused environments."

**Why it's good:**
- Clear expectations
- No hype or false promises
- Sets appropriate candidate filter
- Professional and serious

### 5.5 Email to Prospective Client

**❌ BAD:**
"Hi! Thanks for reaching out! We'd LOVE to chat about how our innovative solutions can transform your business! When can we hop on a quick call to discuss your exciting project? Looking forward to partnering with you!"

**✅ GOOD:**
"Thank you for your inquiry. We have received your request regarding cloud migration services. An engineer will review your requirements and respond within 1-2 business days (IST) to discuss technical approach, timeline, and engagement structure."

**Why it's good:**
- Professional and measured
- Sets clear response expectation
- No false intimacy ("love," "exciting")
- Businesslike without being cold

### 5.6 Compliance Statement

**❌ BAD:**
"We follow all the latest security best practices and are committed to keeping your data safe. Trust us with your most sensitive information!"

**✅ GOOD:**
"Clyros Tech is not certified under SOC 2 or ISO 27001. We implement security controls aligned with these frameworks and can provide documentation of practices during due diligence. Clients retain ultimate accountability for their own compliance obligations."

**Why it's good:**
- Legally precise
- No false certifications
- Clear about limitations
- Sets appropriate responsibility boundaries

## 6. GRAMMAR AND MECHANICS

### 6.1 Punctuation

**Exclamation Marks:**
- AVOID in all external content
- Acceptable only in direct quotes where removal would alter meaning
- Never use multiple (!!!)

**Em Dashes and Colons:**
- Use for clarity and emphasis
- Prefer over parenthetical asides in formal content

**Oxford Comma:**
- ALWAYS use (consistent with AP and Chicago styles)
- Example: "AWS, Azure, and GCP" (NOT "AWS, Azure and GCP")

### 6.2 Capitalization

**Service Names:**
- Capitalize when referring to Clyros Tech service categories
- Example: "Cloud Architecture & Engineering" (on website)
- Example: "cloud architecture services" (in body text)

**Technologies:**
- Follow vendor capitalization (AWS, Azure, PostgreSQL, Kubernetes)

**Job Titles:**
- Capitalize when used as formal title before name
- Lowercase when used descriptively
- Example: "Chief Technology Officer Jane Smith" vs. "our chief technology officer"

### 6.3 Numbers

**When to Use Numerals:**
- Always for percentages (40%, not forty percent)
- Always for measurements (5 years, 100 GB)
- Always in technical contexts (10 microservices, 3 availability zones)

**When to Spell Out:**
- Numbers at beginning of sentences (spell out or restructure)
- Approximate or colloquial usage ("a few engineers" not "a 3 engineers")

### 6.4 Abbreviations and Acronyms

**First Use:**
- Spell out with acronym in parentheses
- Example: "Service Level Agreement (SLA)"

**Subsequent Use:**
- Use acronym without parentheses
- Example: "SLA violations trigger escalation"

**Well-Known Terms:**
- No need to spell out universally known terms: API, CI/CD, AWS, HTTP
- Spell out industry-specific terms that may not be universally known

## 7. CONTENT TYPE-SPECIFIC RULES

### 7.1 Website Content

- Desktop-first writing (longer sentences acceptable)
- Semantic HTML heading hierarchy (H1, H2, H3)
- No pop-ups, urgency timers, or interruptive elements
- Contact forms collect only necessary information

### 7.2 Proposals and RFP Responses

- Follow client-provided structure and format exactly
- Answer questions directly without adding sales language
- Provide verifiable facts and specific examples
- Include caveats and limitations where appropriate

### 7.3 Case Studies

- Do NOT name clients without explicit written permission
- Use anonymized descriptions ("Regional healthcare provider," "Fortune 500 financial services firm")
- Focus on technical challenge and solution, not business outcomes claims
- Include realistic outcomes, not inflated success metrics

### 7.4 Thought Leadership

- Refer to `Thought_Leadership_Guidelines.md` for detailed rules
- No predictions or future-state claims
- Analytical tone, not opinionated or controversial
- Suitable for CXO and regulatory audience

### 7.5 Social Media (if applicable)

- LinkedIn company page only (no other platforms currently)
- Professional updates only (new hires, case studies with permission, insights)
- No promotional posts, memes, or trending hashtag participation
- All posts require Executive Leadership review

## 8. INCLUSIVE AND RESPECTFUL LANGUAGE

### 8.1 Gender-Neutral Language

**Use:**
- "Engineers," "team members," "employees" (not "guys")
- "They/their" as singular pronoun when gender unknown
- Role titles without gendered suffixes ("engineer" not "female engineer")

### 8.2 Respectful Professional Language

**Avoid:**
- Ageist language ("young," "energetic," "digital native")
- Ability-based metaphors ("blind spot," "falling on deaf ears")
- Cultural assumptions (e.g., assuming all clients celebrate same holidays)

**Why:** Inclusive language is both ethically correct and legally prudent. It also expands our talent and client pool.

### 8.3 International Audience Awareness

- Spell out dates (January 5, 2026 NOT 1/5/26 or 5/1/26)
- Include timezone with times (9:00 AM IST, 2:00 PM EST)
- Avoid region-specific idioms ("home run," "cricket score")
- Metric and imperial measurements when appropriate to audience

## 9. ENFORCEMENT AND VIOLATIONS

### 9.1 Content Review Responsibilities

All content creators must:
1. Review this document before creating external content
2. Self-check content against approved/prohibited language
3. Submit content for review per workflow (see `Content_Review_Workflow.md`)
4. Accept and incorporate feedback without argument

### 9.2 Common Violations

**Most Frequent Issues:**
- Using superlatives ("best," "leading," "world-class")
- Vague service descriptions without specifics
- Making certification claims that are false
- Forgetting to submit content for review before publication

**How to Avoid:**
- Use Find & Replace to check for prohibited terms before submission
- Ask "Can I prove this claim?" before making it
- When in doubt, be more conservative
- Reference approved examples in this document

### 9.3 Feedback and Iteration

When content is returned for revision:
- Revisions are NOT optional
- Understand the reason for the change (ask if unclear)
- Apply the lesson to future content
- Do NOT reintroduce removed language in subsequent drafts
