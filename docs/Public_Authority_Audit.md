## Internal vs. Public Separation Guidelines

### ‚úÖ SAFE TO PUBLISH (Public Authority Signals)

**Characteristics:**
- High-level principles and philosophy
- Outcomes and benefits (not internal mechanics)
- Client-facing processes (status reporting, communication cadence)
- Industry best practices and standards awareness
- Anonymized case studies and examples
- Thought leadership and perspectives

**Examples:**
- "We conduct weekly status reporting" ‚úÖ
- "We follow structured delivery methodology with quality gates" ‚úÖ
- "We are HIPAA-aware and design systems to support client compliance" ‚úÖ
- "We maintain engineering discipline through code review and automated testing" ‚úÖ

---

### ‚ùå NEVER PUBLISH (Internal Operating Systems)

**Characteristics:**
- Specific metrics, KPIs, or targets
- Internal decision rights and authority levels
- Escalation procedures and emergency protocols
- Succession planning and backup authority
- Crisis communication playbooks
- M&A readiness and corporate structure
- Brand governance and claims registers
- RFP templates and sales strategies

**Examples:**
- "Our on-time delivery rate is 87%" ‚ùå
- "Decisions under $5K are approved by Delivery Lead" ‚ùå
- "In emergency, Tier 2 authority activates within 1 hour" ‚ùå
- "We maintain 2-3 internal successors for each critical role" ‚ùå
- "Level 4 crises trigger executive notification within 30 minutes" ‚ùå

---

### ‚ö†Ô∏è GRAY AREA (Requires Judgment)

**Characteristics:**
- Aggregated or anonymized data (e.g., "majority of clients are repeat business")
- General governance statements (e.g., "we have board oversight")
- High-level risk management (e.g., "we identify and escalate risks proactively")
- Quality standards (e.g., "code review is mandatory")

**Guideline:**  
If it demonstrates maturity without exposing competitive intelligence or creating client concern, it's likely safe. When in doubt, err on side of confidentiality.
