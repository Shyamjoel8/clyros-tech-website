## Document Purpose

This document defines Clyros Tech's approach to critical role coverage. It provides:
- Framework for identifying critical roles

- Dependency risk awareness and mitigation

## Critical Role Identification

### Definition of Critical Role

**A role is critical if:**
1. **Vacancy creates immediate operational disruption** (decisions delayed, work stopped, clients impacted)
2. **Role holder has unique knowledge or relationships** (difficult to replace quickly)
3. **Role has significant decision-making authority** (financial, client, strategic)
4. **Role has external stakeholder relationships** (clients, investors, board, regulators)

---

### Critical Role Categories

**Category 1: Executive Leadership**

**Characteristics:**
- Strategic direction and decision-making authority
- Client relationships and trust (executive-level)
- External stakeholder communication (investors, board, partners)
- Operational oversight and accountability
- Financial and commercial authority

**Example Roles:**
- Chief Executive Officer (CEO) / Founder
- Chief Technology Officer (CTO) / Technical Lead
- Chief Operating Officer (COO) / Operations Lead
- Chief Financial Officer (CFO) / Finance Lead (if applicable)

**Vacancy Impact:**
- Strategic decisions delayed or blocked
- Client executive relationships at risk
- External stakeholder confidence impacted
- Operational accountability unclear

---

**Category 2: Delivery Leadership**

**Characteristics:**
- Client relationship ownership (operational and delivery)
- Delivery accountability and oversight
- Technical expertise and problem-solving
- Team coordination and mentorship
- Quality and risk management

**Example Roles:**
- Delivery Leads / Engagement Managers (for major clients or engagements)
- Technical Leads / Senior Architects (for complex or specialized work)
- Practice Leads (if applicable)

**Vacancy Impact:**
- Client delivery disrupted or delayed
- Client relationships at risk (if not transitioned properly)
- Team coordination and decision-making impacted
- Quality and risk management gaps

---

**Category 3: Specialized Expertise**

**Characteristics:**
- Rare or specialized technical knowledge
- Single point of expertise (no backup or redundancy)
- Critical to specific engagements or service lines
- Difficult to replace quickly (long ramp-up time)

**Example Roles:**
- Security specialists (if single expert)
- Domain experts (healthcare, financial services, etc.)
- Technology specialists (specific platforms or frameworks)

**Vacancy Impact:**
- Engagements blocked or delayed (if expertise required)
- Quality risk (if expertise not available for review or guidance)
- Client confidence impacted (if expertise was selling point)

---

**Category 4: Governance & Advisory**

**Characteristics:**
- Governance oversight and accountability
- Legal or financial expertise
- External stakeholder relationships
- Strategic guidance and decision-making

**Example Roles:**
- Board of Directors (if applicable)
- Legal Counsel
- Financial Advisors / Accountants
- Strategic Advisors

**Vacancy Impact:**
- Governance oversight gaps
- Legal or financial risk (if expertise not available)
- Strategic guidance unavailable
- External stakeholder relationships disrupted

## Interim Coverage Principles

### Purpose of Interim Coverage

**Interim coverage ensures:**
1. **Immediate continuity** (no delay in critical decisions or operations)
2. **Client reassurance** (clients informed of coverage and continuity)
3. **Team stability** (clear authority and accountability)
4. **Time to find permanent successor** (interim coverage buys time for proper succession process)

---

### Interim Coverage Requirements

**For All Critical Roles:**

**1. Pre-Designated Interim Coverage**
- Identify interim coverage before vacancy occurs (not during crisis)
- Document interim coverage in succession plans
- Communicate interim coverage to relevant stakeholders (Board, Executive Leadership)

**2. Authority Delegation**
- Define decision-making authority for interim coverage (what decisions can be made)
- Document authority delegation in writing (no ambiguity)
- Ensure interim coverage has access to information and resources needed

**3. Knowledge Transfer**
- Document critical knowledge, processes, and relationships
- Provide interim coverage with access to documentation
- Facilitate knowledge transfer before vacancy (if planned transition)

**4. Client Communication**
- Inform clients of interim coverage (reassurance and continuity)
- Introduce interim coverage to key clients (build trust)
- Maintain regular client communication during transition

**5. Timeline**
- Interim coverage is temporary (typically 30-90 days)
- Use interim period to find permanent successor
- Avoid indefinite interim coverage (creates uncertainty and instability)

---

### Interim Coverage Selection Criteria

**Who Should Serve as Interim Coverage:**

**1. Internal Candidate with Relevant Experience**
- Preferred option (understands culture, clients, operations)
- Has relevant experience or expertise (can step in quickly)
- Has capacity to assume additional responsibilities (not overloaded)
- Has trust of team and clients (credibility and confidence)

**2. Peer or Colleague**
- For Delivery Leadership roles: Another Delivery Lead
- For Executive Leadership roles: Another Executive or Board Member
- For Specialized Expertise: Another expert or external consultant

**3. External Interim Executive (If No Internal Option)**
- For Executive Leadership roles: Interim CEO, CTO, COO (if no internal candidate)
- Requires Board approval and engagement process
- Typically more expensive and slower to onboard
- Used when internal capacity insufficient or expertise gap

---

### Interim Coverage Limitations

**Interim coverage is NOT:**
- Guaranteed succession (interim does not automatically become permanent)
- Full replacement (interim may have limited authority or scope)
- Long-term solution (interim is temporary by definition)

**Interim coverage should NOT:**
- Make irreversible strategic decisions (without Board approval)
- Commit to long-term contracts or obligations (without Board approval)
- Make major organizational changes (hiring, firing, restructuring)

**Interim coverage SHOULD:**
- Maintain operations and client delivery (continuity)
- Make routine operational decisions (day-to-day)
- Communicate regularly with Board and Executive Leadership (transparency)
- Facilitate search for permanent successor (support, not obstruct)

## Knowledge Transfer Expectations

### Purpose of Knowledge Transfer

**Knowledge transfer ensures:**
1. **Continuity** (operations continue without disruption)
2. **Risk mitigation** (knowledge is not lost with departure)
3. **Efficiency** (successor ramps up quickly)
4. **Quality** (successor has context and understanding)

---

### Knowledge Transfer Categories

**Category 1: Client Relationships**

**What to Transfer:**
- Client contact information (names, roles, phone, email)
- Client history and context (engagement history, preferences, sensitivities)
- Client expectations and commitments (SLAs, deliverables, timelines)
- Client communication cadence and preferences (weekly calls, email updates, etc.)

**How to Transfer:**
- Document client information in CRM or shared system
- Introduce successor to key clients (calls, meetings, emails)
- Shadow client interactions (successor observes incumbent)
- Gradual handoff (incumbent remains available for questions)

---

**Category 2: Operational Processes**

**What to Transfer:**
- Standard operating procedures (how work is done)
- Decision-making processes (who decides what, how)
- Quality gates and controls (code review, testing, security)
- Escalation paths and protocols (when to escalate, to whom)

**How to Transfer:**
- Document processes in shared system (wiki, documentation)
- Walk through processes with successor (explain rationale and nuances)
- Provide examples and templates (proposals, status reports, contracts)
- Answer questions and clarify (successor may need multiple explanations)

---

**Category 3: Technical Knowledge**

**What to Transfer:**
- System architecture and design (how systems work)
- Technology stack and tools (what is used, why)
- Code and configuration (where it is, how to access)
- Known issues and technical debt (what to watch for)

**How to Transfer:**
- Document architecture and design (diagrams, documentation)
- Code walkthrough (explain key components and logic)
- Pair programming or shadowing (successor observes incumbent)
- Provide access to systems and tools (credentials, permissions)

---

**Category 4: Relationships & Networks**

**What to Transfer:**
- Internal relationships (who to work with, who to trust)
- External relationships (vendors, partners, advisors)
- Informal networks (who knows what, who can help)
- Cultural context (how things really work, unwritten rules)

**How to Transfer:**
- Introduce successor to key contacts (internal and external)
- Explain relationship dynamics and context (history, sensitivities)
- Facilitate networking opportunities (meetings, events, introductions)
- Provide ongoing support and advice (incumbent remains available)

---

### Knowledge Transfer Timeline

**Planned Transition (3-12 Months Notice):**

**Phase 1: Preparation (First 30 Days)**
- Document critical knowledge and relationships
- Identify successor (internal or external)
- Develop transition plan and timeline

**Phase 2: Knowledge Transfer (30-90 Days)**
- Successor shadows incumbent (observe and learn)
- Incumbent introduces successor to clients and stakeholders
- Successor assumes increasing responsibility (gradual handoff)
- Incumbent remains available for questions and support

**Phase 3: Transition (Final 30 Days)**
- Successor assumes full responsibility
- Incumbent transitions out (final handoff)
- Incumbent remains available for questions (limited period)

**Total Timeline:** 3-6 months (typical)

---

**Unplanned Transition (Immediate to 30 Days):**

**Phase 1: Immediate Handoff (First 7 Days)**
- Interim coverage assumes role immediately
- Access critical documentation and systems
- Notify clients and stakeholders
- Stabilize operations

**Phase 2: Accelerated Knowledge Transfer (7-30 Days)**
- Outgoing incumbent provides knowledge transfer (if available and willing)
- Document critical knowledge and relationships (if not already done)
- Identify gaps and address urgently
- Find permanent successor

**Phase 3: Permanent Transition (30-90 Days)**
- Permanent successor identified and onboarded
- Knowledge transfer from interim to permanent (if different)
- Stabilize and normalize operations

**Total Timeline:** 1-3 months (compressed)

## Dependency Risk Awareness

### Types of Dependencies

**1. Single Point of Knowledge**

**Risk:**  
Critical knowledge exists only in one person's head. If that person leaves, knowledge is lost.

**Examples:**
- System architecture or design (no documentation)
- Client history and context (no CRM or notes)
- Specialized technical expertise (no backup or redundancy)
- Vendor or partner relationships (no contact list or context)

**Mitigation:**
- Document critical knowledge (architecture, processes, relationships)
- Cross-train team members (share knowledge and expertise)
- Implement knowledge management systems (wiki, documentation, CRM)
- Conduct regular knowledge transfer sessions (team learning)

---

**2. Single Point of Relationship**

**Risk:**  
Critical relationships exist only with one person. If that person leaves, relationships are at risk.

**Examples:**
- Client executive relationships (CEO to CEO)
- Investor or board relationships (founder to investor)
- Vendor or partner relationships (single point of contact)

**Mitigation:**
- Introduce multiple team members to key relationships (redundancy)
- Document relationship context and history (CRM, notes)
- Build institutional relationships (not just personal)
- Maintain regular communication (do not rely on single person)

---

**3. Single Point of Authority**

**Risk:**  
Critical decisions can only be made by one person. If that person is unavailable, decisions are delayed or blocked.

**Examples:**
- Financial authority (only CEO can approve expenditures)
- Client contract authority (only CEO can sign contracts)
- Technical authority (only CTO can approve architecture decisions)

**Mitigation:**
- Delegate authority to backup (define thresholds and limits)
- Document authority delegation (who can decide what)
- Implement approval workflows (multiple approvers for critical decisions)
- Train backup on decision-making process (context and criteria)

---

**4. Single Point of Expertise**

**Risk:**  
Specialized expertise exists only in one person. If that person leaves, expertise is lost.

**Examples:**
- Security expertise (only one security expert)
- Domain expertise (only one healthcare or financial services expert)
- Technology expertise (only one expert in specific platform or framework)

**Mitigation:**
- Cross-train team members (share expertise)
- Hire or contract backup expertise (redundancy)
- Document expertise (runbooks, guides, checklists)
- Build external network (consultants, advisors, vendors)

---

### Dependency Risk Assessment

**For Each Critical Role, Assess:**

**1. Knowledge Dependency**
- What critical knowledge does role holder have?
- Is knowledge documented?
- Who else has this knowledge (backup)?
- What is risk if knowledge is lost?

**2. Relationship Dependency**
- What critical relationships does role holder have?
- Are relationships documented?
- Who else has these relationships (backup)?
- What is risk if relationships are lost?

**3. Authority Dependency**
- What critical decisions does role holder make?
- Is authority delegated to backup?
- Who else can make these decisions (backup)?
- What is risk if decisions are delayed?

**4. Expertise Dependency**
- What specialized expertise does role holder have?
- Is expertise documented?
- Who else has this expertise (backup)?
- What is risk if expertise is lost?

---

### Dependency Risk Mitigation Plan

**For High-Risk Dependencies:**

**1. Document**
- Create documentation (architecture, processes, relationships, expertise)
- Store in accessible location (wiki, CRM, shared drive)
- Keep documentation current (review and update regularly)

**2. Cross-Train**
- Share knowledge with team members (training, shadowing, pair work)
- Build redundancy (multiple people with knowledge or expertise)
- Rotate responsibilities (prevent single-person dependencies)

**3. Delegate Authority**
- Define backup authority (who decides what in absence of primary)
- Document delegation (written, clear, accessible)
- Train backup (context, criteria, process)

**4. Build External Network**
- Identify external resources (consultants, advisors, vendors)
- Maintain relationships (regular communication, contracts)
- Test external resources (ensure they can deliver if needed)

## Coverage Plan Documentation

### What to Document

**For Each Critical Role:**

**1. Role Information**
- Role title and description
- Current incumbent (name, contact information)
- Criticality assessment (why role is critical)

**2. Interim Coverage**
- Primary interim coverage (name, role)
- Backup interim coverage (if primary unavailable)
- Authority delegation (what decisions interim can make)
- Timeline (how long interim coverage expected)

**3. Knowledge Transfer Requirements**
- Critical knowledge to transfer (clients, processes, technical, relationships)
- Documentation status (what is documented, what needs documentation)
- Transfer timeline (planned transition vs. unplanned)

**4. Dependency Risks**
- Knowledge dependencies (single points of knowledge)
- Relationship dependencies (single points of relationship)
- Authority dependencies (single points of authority)
- Expertise dependencies (single points of expertise)
- Mitigation actions (what is being done to reduce risk)

---

### Where to Document

**Coverage Plans:**
- Documented in secure, confidential files
- Stored with controlled access (Board, Executive Leadership only)
- Updated annually (minimum) and after significant changes

**Knowledge Transfer Documentation:**
- Stored in accessible location (wiki, CRM, shared drive)
- Accessible to relevant team members (not just leadership)
- Kept current (review and update regularly)

**Authority Delegation:**
- Documented in Authority Delegation Matrix (separate document)
- Accessible to relevant stakeholders (Board, Executive Leadership, interim coverage)
- Updated when roles or responsibilities change
